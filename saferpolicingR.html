Special Prosecutors and Penalizing False Reports
Would Make Pennsylvania Safer

Medical Students for Safer Policing urge Pennsylvania lawmakers to pass HB1382 and SB1198.

Communities are losing trust in police.

Many high-profile encounters between police and community members — including in Pennsylvania — have damaged our trust in police. For example, in 2018, the police were called on a group of Black women in Dover who were “golfing too slow.”

Deadly police shootings since 2015. Source: The Washington Post.

Since 2015, police have killed 111 people in Pennsylvania, who will no longer contribute to their families and communities. Yet despite evidence of wrongdoing in many cases, no officers have been convicted, and few police departments have undergone reform.

We can enhance trust in police.

Police can be safer and more effective. About two-thirds of Americans do not believe that police use the right amount of force, treat racial groups equally, or hold “bad” police accountable. As future physicians, we know how essential safe communities and positive experiences with institutions are to the health of patients and communities.

Medicine can be a model for police reform. In medicine, deadly errors happen. When they do, we don’t blame the doctor. We examine the system. We work to prevent the conditions that produce errors in the future. If doctors commit gross negligence, they are held responsible. This process improves the system. So, too, can we improve policing.

We urge the passage of HB1382 and SB1198.

HB1382 allows the state to appoint special
prosecutors for deadly force cases.

Impact of this bill:

  • Encourage investigations of conditions that turn deadly.
  • Promote necessary reform by police departments.
  • Bring peace and closure to families of victims.

Common misconceptions:

Myth: “Accountability” for police puts them and us at risk.
Truth: States with special prosecutors have not experienced increases in crime or harm to police during encounters.

Myth: People in my district do not want special prosecutors.
Truth: Two-thirds of Americans believe that civilians should be able to sue officers for misconduct.

Myth: This issue does not affect people in my district.
Truth: The rate of fatal police shootings per crime is 4x higher in small towns than in large cities.

SB1198 elevates false criminal reports based
on race to a first-class misdemeanor.

Impact of this bill:

  • Save police resources by reducing frivolous calls.
  • Increase efficacy of police by focusing on real crime.
  • Prevent unnecessary traumatic encounters with police.

Common misconceptions:

Myth: People will be deterred from calling 911 for real crimes.
Truth: States with special prosecutors have not experienced increases in crime or harm to police during encounters.

Myth: People in my district do not want special prosecutors.
Truth: Two-thirds of Americans believe that civilians should be able to sue officers for misconduct.

Myth: This issue does not affect people in my district.
Truth: The rate of fatal police shootings per crime is 4x higher in small towns than in large cities.
